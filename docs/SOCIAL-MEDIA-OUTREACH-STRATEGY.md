## 1. Executive Summary

QuickTow and other competitors buy relationships with gifts and vacations. Axle Towing competes on professionalism, reliability, and value. The social media strategy reflects this: show up consistently, add value, and build trust before asking for business.

**Primary objective:** Generate 3–5 qualified meetings per week from social media outreach within 90 days.

**Platform priority:**

1. **LinkedIn** — Primary. Best channel for property managers, portfolio managers, and regional corporate reps at companies like Greystar.
2. **Facebook HOA Groups** — Secondary. HOA board members congregate in community groups. Infiltrate by adding value, not selling.
3. **Instagram** — Brand awareness and employer branding. Low-effort content that makes Axle look professional and established.

## 2. Target Persona Profiles

### Persona A: HOA Board President / Community Chair

**Demographics:**

- Age: 45–70
- Occupation: Often retired or semi-retired professional; serves on HOA board voluntarily
- Income: Middle to upper-middle class homeowner
- Location: Gated communities in Scottsdale, Chandler, Gilbert, Mesa, Tempe, Peoria

**Platform behavior:**

- Active on Facebook; in neighborhood and HOA-specific Facebook groups
- Light LinkedIn presence (may have a profile but not active)
- May use Nextdoor but that's not a campaign channel

**Pain points:**

- Gets calls and texts from residents about parking at all hours
- Worried about liability when a tow goes wrong
- Sick of hearing about the same violations at every board meeting
- Previous towing companies were unreliable or hard to reach

**What they want to see from Axle:**

- Evidence that Axle knows HOA law (ARS § 33-1818, ARS § 28-4843)
- Documentation and protection — "we'll cover you if a resident disputes the tow"
- A real person they can call, not a call center
- Stories from other HOAs who solved the same problem

**Best channel:** Facebook HOA groups → DM after establishing trust

### Persona B: Professional Property Manager / Community Association Manager (CAM)

**Demographics:**

- Age: 28–55
- Occupation: Full-time property management professional; typically holds a CAM license in Arizona
- Income: $55,000–$95,000 (salary varies by portfolio size)
- Employer: Regional or national property management companies (Greystar, CCMC, Associa, First Service Residential, Mark-Taylor, AAM)

**Platform behavior:**

- Active on LinkedIn — uses it professionally, likely in industry groups
- May follow property management influencers or CAM associations
- Checks LinkedIn at least 2–3x per week

**Pain points:**

- Managing multiple properties means inconsistent towing vendors with different quality
- Spends too much time on parking disputes that should be the towing company's problem
- Gets pressure from clients (HOA boards) to resolve parking issues faster
- Needs documentation for legal protection when residents dispute tows

**What they want to see from Axle:**

- One point of contact across multiple properties
- Portfolio-level reporting and documentation
- Proof that Axle handles resident complaints directly (saves them time)
- Companies with other CAMs as clients (social proof from peers)

**Best channel:** LinkedIn (InMail, connection requests, content engagement)

### Persona C: Regional Corporate Rep / Director of Property Management

**Demographics:**

- Age: 35–55
- Occupation: Senior manager overseeing 10–30+ communities across Phoenix metro
- Title: Regional Property Manager, Director of Operations, VP of Property Management, Portfolio Director
- Employer: Greystar, CBRE Residential, JLL, Lincoln Property, FirstService Residential

**Platform behavior:**

- Very active on LinkedIn; uses it for hiring, networking, and vendor discovery
- May be a LinkedIn content creator or industry commenter
- Attends CAM Association (CACM / CAI) events

**Pain points:**

- Vendor management at scale — needs towing vendor that can handle 20+ properties consistently
- Board compliance issues ripple up to them when property managers fail to resolve them
- High employee turnover means towing vendors keep losing their relationship contacts
- Cost control — any contract they sign has to justify ROI across a portfolio

**What they want to see from Axle:**

- Scalability: "Can you handle 15 properties with the same quality?"
- Consolidated reporting — single billing and reporting across portfolio
- Reference from another regional director (highest trust)
- No renegotiation needed per-property — one master service agreement

**Best channel:** LinkedIn (Sales Navigator targeting, shared content, event follow-ups)

### 3B. Facebook HOA Groups — Secondary Channel

**Strategy overview:**
Facebook HOA groups are where board members vent, ask questions, and seek recommendations. The goal is to become the trusted resource in these spaces before ever mentioning Axle Towing's name.

**Phase 1 (Weeks 1–4): Join and observe**

- Join 8–12 Phoenix-area HOA and community Facebook groups (see FACEBOOK-HOA-GROUPS-STRATEGY.md)
- Do NOT post or comment for the first week — read what people are talking about
- Identify recurring pain points: parking complaints, tow disputes, vendor frustrations

**Phase 2 (Weeks 5–8): Add value**

- Comment helpfully on posts where you have relevant knowledge (ARS regulations, documentation tips, parking enforcement)
- Answer questions about towing law without being promotional
- Share useful resources (ARS statutes, signage requirements) without linking to Axle

**Phase 3 (Weeks 9+): Soft introduction**

- Post original educational content ("The 3 signs your HOA needs a better parking enforcement process")
- Mention Axle Towing only after building credibility as a knowledgeable community member
- Respond to direct "towing company recommendation" requests with a brief, non-salesy mention

## 4. Content Pillars

Use these five content pillars to rotate posts across all platforms. Every piece of content should serve one of these pillars.

### Pillar 1: HOA Law & Compliance Education

**Examples:**

- "What ARS § 28-4843 actually requires for HOA parking enforcement signage"
- "5 documentation mistakes that expose HOAs to wrongful tow claims"
- "Arizona HOAs: here's your legal authority to enforce parking (ARS § 33-1818)"

**Why it works:** Positions Axle as the expert. Decision makers share educational content. Builds trust before the pitch.

---

### Pillar 2: Case Studies & Social Proof

**Examples:**

- "How a 200-unit Scottsdale HOA eliminated parking complaints in 30 days"
- "Property manager at Mark-Taylor: 'I used to spend 4 hours a month on towing disputes. Now it's 20 minutes.'"
- "Before/after: What documented tow files look like (and why it matters)"

**Why it works:** Proof over promises. Specific numbers and named results convert skeptics.

---

### Pillar 3: Behind-the-Scenes Reliability

**Examples:**

- "3 AM dispatch call — here's what our process looks like" (photo of the truck responding)
- "Elliott's direct cell vs. a call center: here's the difference" (explainer post)
- "A look at our full documentation packet after every tow"

**Why it works:** Shows, doesn't tell. Counters the "all towing companies are the same" objection.

---

### Pillar 4: Industry News & Trends

**Examples:**

- "Phoenix HOA parking enforcement issues are rising — here's why"
- "New Maricopa County development means more HOA communities launching in 2026"
- "CAI Arizona chapter update: What property managers need to know"

**Why it works:** Builds credibility as an industry participant, not just a vendor. Increases LinkedIn algorithm reach.

---

### Pillar 5: Personality & Culture

**Examples:**

- Photos of the team on a job
- Elliott's personal take on what reliability means in this industry
- "We just finished our 1,000th HOA service call" milestone post

**Why it works:** B2B buyers buy from people they trust. Personality content builds that connection.

## 6. Connection/Follow Request Templates

See full templates in `docs/LINKEDIN-OUTREACH-TEMPLATES.md`.

**General rule:** Every connection request must be personalized. Never use LinkedIn's default "I'd like to connect" message. Character limit is 300 characters — be concise.

**Framework:**

1. Reference how you found them (mutual connection, searched their company, saw their comment on a post)
2. One sentence of relevant context (what you do, why it's relevant to them)
3. No ask — just connection

## 7. DM Outreach Sequences

### LinkedIn DM Sequence (post-connection)

**Day 0:** Connect (personalized request — see templates)
**Day 3–5:** Welcome DM (do not pitch — acknowledge the connection, reference their work)
**Day 10–14:** Value message (share a useful resource, educational post, or relevant case study)
**Day 21–28:** Soft ask (offer free signage audit, free consultation, or a quick call)
**Day 45:** Follow-up or break-up message

### Facebook DM Sequence (HOA groups)

Only DM after:

- You've commented helpfully on 2+ of their posts in a group, OR
- They've responded positively to a group comment you made

**Step 1:** Reference the specific group interaction in your DM opening
**Step 2:** Brief value statement — what you do and why it's relevant to them
**Step 3:** No-pressure offer — "Happy to share what we've seen work for HOAs your size"

## 12. Competitive Positioning

QuickTow uses gifts and "backhand stuff" to win accounts. That's a vulnerability, not a strength.

**Counter-positioning:**

- "We earn accounts the same way we keep them — by showing up."
- Never mention competitors by name. Position against "the other guys" behavior generically.
- Use LinkedIn content to contrast value-based service with transactional vendor relationships

**Sample content angle:** "Some towing companies win contracts with steakhouse dinners. We win them by answering the phone at 3 AM. That's also why we keep them." → strong LinkedIn post that resonates with professional property managers who've been burned by unreliable vendors.
