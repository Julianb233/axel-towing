## Executive Summary

Private property towing in Phoenix metro is in the middle of its biggest regulatory shift in fifteen years. Arizona House Bill 2305, signed in 2024, consolidated what had been a patchwork of city-, county-, and town-level tow rules into a single statewide framework. The transition has surfaced two important truths for property managers:

1. **Most master vendor agreements written before 2024 are now technically out of compliance** — usually due to outdated signage specs, fee schedules tied to local ordinances that no longer exist, or CC&R references that point to repealed code sections.

2. **Phoenix's unique combination of high housing growth, transient population, and aging multifamily stock has produced the highest abandoned-vehicle rate per 10,000 units of any major Sun Belt metro.** [DATA PLACEHOLDER — ELLIOTT TO VERIFY: Brian's industry estimate. Quantify if possible.]

This report draws on operational data from over 60 Phoenix-metro multifamily and HOA communities Axle Towing serves, public records from the Arizona Department of Transportation (ADOT), Maricopa County code compliance reports, and U.S. Census American Community Survey data on Phoenix housing.

The report is intended for property managers, HOA boards, commercial real estate operators, and policymakers. It's not legal advice and should not be relied on as such. We're a tow yard — we know what comes through our gate. That's a worthwhile data set, but it's not the whole picture.

## 2. The HB2305 Effect: One Year Later

Arizona House Bill 2305, signed in 2024 and effective late 2024, prohibits counties, cities, and towns from setting their own private property tow rates or rules. Arizona is now under a single statewide framework for:

- **Tow company licensing** (formerly varied by jurisdiction)
- **Maximum fees** (now set at state level)
- **Signage specifications** (formerly varied by city)
- **Vehicle owner notification timelines** (now uniform)
- **Lien filing process** (always state-level, but now harmonized)

### What this means in practice

Before HB2305, a property in Mesa had different signage rules than a property in Phoenix, which had different rules than a property in unincorporated Maricopa County. Property managers running portfolios that crossed jurisdictions had to maintain three or four separate compliance programs.

That's gone. One framework, one set of signage specs, one fee schedule, one notification timeline.

### What property managers should do (most aren't yet)

[DATA POINT — verify with Elliott: in Q1 2026 audits Axle ran for [N] portfolios, ___% had at least one signage gap relative to the new framework. Most common issues were:]

1. **Signage that referenced a city ordinance that no longer applies.** Most properties used pre-printed signs with municipal code references. Those references are now stale.
2. **Fee schedules in master vendor contracts indexed to local rate caps.** With caps now state-set, those contract clauses are unenforceable.
3. **CC&Rs (HOAs especially) that reference "applicable city regulations."** Now needs to read "applicable Arizona statutes" to align with HB2305.

The fix is straightforward — typically a one-time signage swap and a vendor agreement amendment — but it requires intentional review. Properties that haven't audited their tow program post-HB2305 are operating with technical compliance gaps.

### 3b. Climate-driven vehicle abandonment

Arizona summers create vehicle abandonment patterns not seen in cooler metros. A car with a dead battery left in 115°F heat for 2 weeks is often beyond owner-recovery economics — battery, tires, interior plastics all degrade fast. Owners more often choose to abandon than recover.

Operationally this shows up as:
- Higher Q3 abandonment rates than Q1/Q4
- Higher per-tow recovery costs (more vehicles unsalvageable)

## 5. The HOA Story: Boards Are Getting More Confident

[DATA PLACEHOLDER]

HOA tow events as a percentage of Axle's volume rose from ___% in 2024 to ___% in 2025 — and Q1 2026 trended even higher.

Three factors:

1. **HB2305 gave boards a clear, statewide framework to point to.** Before, board members worried about being sued by a homeowner whose vehicle was towed. The clear statewide statute makes that liability much harder to argue.

2. **CAI (Community Associations Institute) educational programming has been pushing CC&R enforcement consistency.** Boards that previously enforced inconsistently are now formalizing parking rules.

3. **Aging communities are seeing more abandoned vehicles.** As Phoenix metro HOAs age past 25 years, original owners die, transfer, or stop paying dues — and their vehicles sometimes stay parked.

### What HOAs are getting wrong

- **Posting too many warnings before towing.** Compliant signage gives immediate authority under ARS 28-3511. Boards that send weeks of warning letters undermine their own signage authority.
- **Inconsistent enforcement.** Towing one vehicle but not another for the same violation opens discrimination claims. Either tow all violations or document a clear, defensible reason.
- **Confusing "abandoned" with "unauthorized."** Two different statutes, two different processes. Most board "abandoned vehicle" complaints are actually unauthorized parking — and easier to handle.

## 6. The Apartment Story: Lease-Tow Alignment Is Still Fuzzy

- What happens if a vehicle becomes inoperable mid-lease
- What happens if a vehicle's registration expires mid-lease
- What permit or sticker is required and where it must be displayed

## 7. What's Coming in 12 Months

### Likely

- **Continued enforcement consolidation.** More properties will move from "we'll pick a vendor when we need one" to portfolio-level master agreements with one operator.
- **Pickup rate compression.** Inflation + economic stress on lower-income vehicle owners will push pickup rates down 3-5 points across the industry. Towing companies that depend on auction recovery will exit.
- **More HOA tow events.** CAI educational push + HB2305 clarity = continued upward trend.

### Possible

- **Cap-rate adjustments.** State legislature may revisit fee caps in 2026-2027 session. Tow companies are lobbying for indexed-to-inflation, consumer advocacy groups are lobbying for caps.
- **More municipal pushback on HB2305.** Some cities (Tucson notably) have signaled they'd like to reclaim local rule-setting authority. Watch for legal challenges.

### Unlikely (but worth flagging)

- **A return to municipal-level tow rule patchwork.** Politically and operationally, this is hard to imagine.
- **Substantial drop in tow volume.** Phoenix's housing growth alone keeps the long-run trend up.

## 8. Recommendations for Property Managers

### Audit your signage post-HB2305 (high priority, easy fix)

If you haven't done a signage audit since HB2305 took effect, you have at least one compliance gap somewhere in your portfolio. Most are easily fixed with a sign swap. Cost: ~$150-300 per property.

### Standardize your vendor agreement template

Most master vendor agreements written before 2024 reference local rate caps, local signage specs, and local permit codes that no longer apply. A 1-page amendment realigning to state framework is standard.

### Get quarterly tow logs from your vendor

Volume, reasons, statute-cited compliance summaries. If your current vendor isn't providing these, you're missing the documentation you'd need if a tow becomes a legal dispute.

### Align your lease/CC&R language to the framework

For apartments: 1-page lease addendum.
For HOAs: 1-page CC&R amendment or board policy statement clarifying parking enforcement procedure.

### Don't underestimate the reputational angle

Tenants and homeowners DO talk to each other about towing experiences. The properties that maintain trust through this regulatory transition are the ones with consistent enforcement, clear signage, and a vendor who handles disputes professionally rather than escalating.
