## <a id="what-free-means"></a>What "Free" Actually Means

The phrase "free private property towing" surprises every property manager who hears it for the first time, so it is worth being precise about what it does and does not include.

**What is free for the property owner:**

- **No setup or onboarding fee.** Site survey, signage recommendations, contract preparation, and account creation are all included.
- **No per-tow charge.** Each individual removal — abandoned vehicle, fire-lane violation, unauthorized resident parking — costs the property zero.
- **No monthly minimum.** Some lots only request one tow per quarter. Others request several per week. Either is fine, and both are free to the property.
- **No equipment cost.** Signage installation, parking-violation door hangers, and tow-request authorization forms are provided.
- **No staff training fee.** We meet on-site with maintenance, leasing, security, or board members and walk through the request process.

**What the vehicle owner pays at retrieval:**

When a vehicle is towed from your property, the registered owner of that vehicle pays standard towing and storage fees directly to the impound yard when they retrieve it. Those fees are governed by Arizona law (ARS 28-3511) and are entirely between the towing company and the vehicle owner. The property owner is not involved in collection, billing, or dispute. The vehicle owner is also responsible for proving ownership and presenting valid identification before the vehicle is released.

This model is sometimes called "private property impound" (PPI) and it is how every legitimate private property towing company in Arizona operates. The economic logic is simple: vehicles parked in violation of posted rules are paid for by the people who left them there, not by the people whose property they are blocking.

If a tow company in Arizona quotes a property owner a setup fee, a per-tow fee, or a monthly retainer for standard private property impound work, that is a strong signal to look elsewhere. The state law structure was specifically designed so legitimate property enforcement is cost-neutral for the property.

## <a id="setup-steps"></a>Step-by-Step Setup (7 Steps, 7–14 Days)

Most properties move from first phone call to active service within seven to fourteen days. Here is the exact sequence.

### Step 1: Audit Your Existing Signage

The single most common reason a private property tow gets disputed in Arizona is a signage gap. Before anything else, walk every vehicle entrance to the property and confirm that signs are present, visible from at least fifty feet, and contain the towing company name, the towing company phone number, and the storage facility address. Older signs frequently list a previous towing company or a disconnected number, and those will not pass a compliant check.

If the property has no signs at all, that is fine — Step 3 covers installation. The audit is to give your assessor a starting picture.

### Step 2: Schedule a Free Property Assessment

Call dispatch at **(480) 288-5526** or use the [Get a Free Quote](https://axletowing.com/contact) form. Our assessor visits the property within five business days, walks the lot with you, and produces a written summary of:

- Total square footage and parking-stall count
- Number of vehicle entrances and how many signs each requires
- Existing signage condition and any required replacements
- Specific enforcement targets you have identified (fire lanes, residential-only, no-overnight, no-RV)
- Recommended response posture (on-call only, scheduled patrols, hybrid)

The assessment costs nothing, includes no obligation, and produces a written document the property keeps.

### Step 3: Sign the Letter of Authorization

Arizona private property towing requires a written contract or letter of authorization between the property owner and the towing company. Ours is a one-page letter that names the property, lists the authorized representatives who may request a tow, identifies the towing company, and is signed by the owner or the owner's agent. There are no minimums, no exclusivity terms, and no cancellation fees.

Property management companies typically sign one master agreement that covers every property in their portfolio, with property-specific addenda for each individual location.

### Step 4: Posted Signage Installed

We install ARS 9-499.05-compliant signs at every vehicle entrance to the property within five business days of contract signing. Standard signage includes the property name, the towing company name and number, and the storage facility address. Custom additions — "Resident Permit Parking Only," "Fire Lane Tow Away Zone," "Visitor Spaces 1–6 Only," "Two-Hour Customer Parking" — are added at no additional cost as part of the property's specific enforcement plan. ### Step 5: Train Your Authorized Representatives

We meet on-site with whoever will be calling in tow requests — leasing managers, on-site maintenance, security, the HOA community manager, the board's parking committee — and walk through the request process. Each authorized representative receives a personal authorization code that identifies them when they call dispatch. The training session is typically 30–45 minutes and can be combined with your next staff meeting or board meeting.

### Step 6: Activate 24/7 Dispatch

Once signage is installed and at least one authorized representative is trained, the account is live. Calls to **(480) 288-5526** are routed to a real dispatcher (no voicemail tree) at any hour. Your authorized rep gives their code, identifies the property and the violation, and our truck is dispatched. Typical response time across Maricopa County is 25–35 minutes.

The first request is the one that confirms the system is working. ## <a id="signage-basics"></a>Signage Compliance Basics

Posted signage is the legal foundation of private property towing in Arizona. Without a compliant sign at every vehicle entrance, no tow from the property is enforceable, and disputes from vehicle owners become much harder to defend.

The three core requirements that property owners need to know about — without getting deep into the statutory text — are:

1. **Visibility.** Signs must be readable from at least fifty feet away under normal lighting. Faded paint, obstructions, and signs that are too small for the entrance count as non-compliant.
2. **Required content.** Every sign must list the towing company name, the towing company phone number, and the storage facility address.
3. **Placement.** A sign at every vehicle entrance to the property — not just one on a wall inside the parking area. Multiple entrances mean multiple signs.

## <a id="first-30-days"></a>What to Expect in Your First 30 Days

The first month of a new private property towing program is the period most property managers worry about. Here is the realistic picture, drawn from hundreds of new partner properties.

**Week 1 — Awareness.** Your residents, tenants, customers, or visitors will notice the new signs. Some will ask questions; a few will complain. Have your communication ready: a paragraph that explains the program, a reference to the parking rules, and a contact (you, the manager, or the HOA board) for follow-up questions. Most properties handle this with a single tenant email or a board-meeting announcement.

**Week 2 — Voluntary compliance.** Many of the worst offenders self-correct in the first week or two without a single tow. They see the new signs, recognize the change, and start parking where they are supposed to. The deterrent effect of professional signage alone is significant.

**Weeks 3–4 — First active enforcement.** The remaining offenders are the ones who require an actual tow. This is when the first one or two requests typically happen. Each tow is logged, photographed, and documented. The vehicle owner contacts the impound yard, not your office.

**By Day 30 — Steady state.** Most properties report a 60–80% reduction in unauthorized parking complaints by the end of the first month. The remaining issues are the ones that need ongoing enforcement (a chronic visitor who keeps coming back, a known abandoned vehicle that finally gets removed, a delivery truck blocking a fire lane during a busy season).

A common mistake is calling in too many requests in the first week. Unless the property has a serious safety violation (a vehicle in a fire lane, blocking a dumpster, blocking an emergency egress, parked in a posted handicap space without a placard), most issues benefit from a 24-hour grace window. This protects the property's reputation with residents and gives chronic offenders the chance to self-correct before escalation.

Properties that hit a steady cadence typically average two to four tows per month for a 200-unit apartment, three to ten per quarter for a typical HOA, and one to three per month for a mid-sized commercial center. Higher numbers are common during seasonal events — the first week of an apartment move-in season, an HOA's annual meeting, a retail center's holiday weekend.

## <a id="choose-partner"></a>How to Choose a Towing Partner — Criteria & Red Flags

Once a property is committed to setting up enforcement, the only real decision is which towing company to partner with. Here is what experienced property managers evaluate.

### Criteria That Matter

- **24/7 dispatch with a real person.** Voicemail trees, weekend voicemail, and "we'll call you back" are deal-breakers. Someone on the phone, day or night, is the baseline.
- **Response-time guarantee.** A written commitment to a target response time (we use 30 minutes across the Phoenix metro) gives the property a service-level standard to evaluate against.
- **Two yards minimum, in opposing directions.** A single yard becomes a single point of failure if it gets full or has access issues. Axle operates yards in both Phoenix (East side) and Apache Junction (East Valley).
- **Insurance and licensing.** Arizona DPS-licensed, fully insured, with documentation available on request.
- **Trade association membership.** AMA Phoenix (Arizona Multihousing Association), BOMA Arizona (Building Owners and Managers Association), and CAI Arizona (Community Associations Institute) memberships indicate ongoing industry engagement.
- **Documentation discipline.** Pre-tow and post-tow photos, vehicle owner notification logs, chain-of-custody records. If a tow is challenged, this is the property's defense.
- **Bilingual capability.** Phoenix is a bilingual market. Spanish-language driver communication and vehicle-owner notification reduce friction at retrieval.

### Red Flags to Walk Away From

- **Any setup fee, retainer, or per-tow charge.** This is not how legitimate private property towing operates in Arizona.
- **No physical office address.** Mobile-only operations without a permanent yard are not equipped to handle disputes or compliant storage.
- **Refusal to commit to response time.** "We'll get there when we get there" is not a service standard.
- **Signage that lists their own ownership but not your property's name.** Compliant signs can list the towing company; they should also be tailored to the specific property where they are installed.
- **Pressure tactics during the sales conversation.** A reputable provider will offer the assessment, deliver a written summary, and let the property decide on its own timeline.
- **Negative trade-association standing.** A simple call to AMA Phoenix or your local property management organization will surface known bad actors.
