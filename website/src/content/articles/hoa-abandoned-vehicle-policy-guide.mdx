# HOA Abandoned Vehicle Policy Guide for Arizona Communities

Abandoned vehicles create one of the most visible parking problems inside an HOA community. They sit in guest areas, overflow spaces, curbside zones, or common parking areas long enough that residents start asking whether the board is enforcing the rules at all.

An effective abandoned vehicle policy gives the board, community manager, and towing partner a shared playbook. It does not need to be complicated. It needs to be clear, documented, and easy to follow.

## What the policy should answer

Your HOA abandoned vehicle policy should answer five operational questions:

1. What counts as an abandoned or stored vehicle?
2. Who can report the vehicle?
3. Who verifies the issue?
4. What documentation is required?
5. When should the towing partner be contacted?

The goal is not to create a legal argument with residents. The goal is to make the parking process predictable.

## Common signs of an abandoned vehicle

HOA boards and managers often flag vehicles that show one or more of these patterns:

- the vehicle has not moved for an extended period
- tags are expired
- tires are flat
- the vehicle is visibly damaged or inoperable
- the vehicle is taking a guest or common-area space long term
- residents do not know who owns it
- management has received repeated complaints

Each community should apply its own CC&Rs, parking rules, and manager guidance. Axle Towing can support the operational side once the community has a clear enforcement path.

## Why documentation matters

Documentation keeps the process fair. It also helps the board show that parking enforcement is not selective.

At minimum, keep:

- date and time observed
- vehicle make, model, color, and plate if visible
- location on property
- photos
- resident or manager notes
- communication with the towing provider

This creates a clean internal record and reduces confusion later.
