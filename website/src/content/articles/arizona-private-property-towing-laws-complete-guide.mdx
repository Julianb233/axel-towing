# Arizona Private Property Towing Laws: Complete 2026 Guide

Arizona private property towing is governed by a combination of state statutes, municipal codes, and recent legislation. Whether you are a property owner authorizing tows, a property manager running a parking enforcement program, or a vehicle owner whose car was towed, understanding these laws protects your rights and ensures legal compliance.

This guide covers every Arizona law that applies to private property towing, organized by audience and topic. All statute references are current as of 2026.

## Key Statutes at a Glance

| Statute | Official Title | What It Covers |
|---------|---------------|---------------|
| ARS 9-499.05 | Municipal regulation of towing | How cities regulate towing from private property; signage, fees, release requirements |
| ARS 28-3511 | Immobilization and impoundment | Law enforcement-ordered vehicle impounds (DUI, suspended license, etc.) |
| ARS 28-3512 | Post-impound hearings | Vehicle owner right to request a hearing after impound |

## Part 1: What Property Owners and Managers Need to Know

### Your Right to Authorize Towing

Under Arizona law, private property owners have the right to authorize the removal of unauthorized vehicles from their property. This right is fundamental to property management and applies to:

- Apartment complexes and multifamily housing
- Homeowner association (HOA) common areas and parking lots
- Commercial properties (retail, office, medical, industrial)
- Restaurant and hospitality parking lots
- Church and religious facility parking
- Any privately owned parking area

However, this right comes with specific legal obligations. Failure to comply with these requirements can result in liability for the property owner, the property manager, and the towing company.

### ARS 9-499.05: The Core Private Property Towing Statute

ARS 9-499.05 is the primary Arizona statute governing private property towing. It grants municipalities the authority to regulate towing from private property within their jurisdictions. Here is what it requires:

#### Signage Requirements

**This is the most frequently violated requirement and the one most likely to create legal liability.**

Before any vehicle can be legally towed from private property in Arizona, the property must have signs that:

1. **Are posted at every entrance** to the property or parking area
2. **Are clearly visible** and not obstructed by landscaping, structures, or other signs
3. **State in clear language** that unauthorized vehicles will be towed at the vehicle owner's expense
4. **Include the name and telephone number** of the towing company authorized to remove vehicles
5. **Meet minimum size requirements** established by the local jurisdiction or HB 2269

A tow performed without compliant signage can be challenged as an illegal tow, exposing the property owner and towing company to financial liability.

#### Fee Regulations

ARS 9-499.05 allows municipalities to set maximum towing and storage fees. Key provisions include:

- Fees must be **reasonable** and not excessive
- The towing company must provide an **itemized receipt** detailing all charges
- The towing company must accept **cash, debit cards, and credit cards** for payment
- Some municipalities set specific dollar caps on tow charges and daily storage rates

#### 24-Hour Release Requirement

**This is a non-negotiable legal requirement.** Under Arizona law:

- Impound lots must make vehicles available for release **24 hours a day, 7 days a week**
- The towing company cannot refuse to release a vehicle because it is "after hours" or a weekend/holiday
- An after-hours release fee may be charged, but the release itself cannot be denied
- The vehicle must be released to the registered owner (or authorized person) upon payment of all applicable fees and presentation of valid identification

#### Law Enforcement Notification

After towing a vehicle from private property, the towing company is required to notify local law enforcement within a reasonable time. This notification:

- Creates an official record of the tow
- Allows police to update their stolen vehicle database (to avoid confusion when the vehicle owner reports it "stolen")
- Provides a paper trail for dispute resolution
- Is typically done via electronic reporting systems in the Phoenix metro area

### HB 2269: Updated Signage Requirements

House Bill 2269 updated and clarified the signage requirements for private property towing in Arizona. These updated requirements took effect and establish:

#### Sign Content Requirements

Every private property towing sign must include:

1. **The words "Tow-Away Zone" or equivalent** in prominent lettering
2. **The name of the towing company** authorized to tow from the property
3. **The telephone number of the towing company** (must be accessible 24/7)
4. **A statement that unauthorized vehicles will be towed at the vehicle owner's expense**
5. **The address of the impound lot** where towed vehicles will be stored (some jurisdictions)

#### Sign Size and Placement Requirements

- **Minimum sign dimensions:** Specified by local ordinance, but generally no smaller than 12" x 18"
- **Letter height:** Key text (especially "Tow-Away Zone") must be legible from the property entrance
- **Placement height:** Signs must be posted at a height visible to drivers entering the property (typically 4-7 feet from ground level)
- **Location:** At every vehicle entrance to the property or parking area
- **Illumination:** Signs must be readable at all times, including at night (either illuminated or made of reflective material)

#### Common Signage Mistakes That Create Legal Liability

| Mistake | Risk Level | Consequence |
|---------|:----------:|------------|
| No signs posted at all | Critical | Tow is illegal; full liability for property owner |
| Signs too small to read | High | Tow can be challenged as improper notice |
| Signs missing towing company name/number | High | Does not meet ARS 9-499.05 requirements |
| Signs blocked by landscaping or other objects | Medium | Notice considered ineffective |
| Signs only at some entrances, not all | Medium | Vehicles entering through unsigned entrance have a defense |
| Signs referencing a different towing company than the one performing tows | High | Authorization chain is broken |
| Outdated signs (previous towing company listed) | High | Notice is inaccurate; tow can be challenged |

### Setting Up a Legal Towing Program

To establish a legally compliant private property towing program in Arizona, property owners and managers must follow these steps:

**Step 1: Select a licensed towing company.** Verify that the company holds a valid Arizona tow truck license under ARS 28-4841. Confirm they carry appropriate insurance and are familiar with ARS 9-499.05 requirements.

**Step 2: Execute a written authorization agreement.** Document the scope of towing authorization, including which vehicles may be towed (unauthorized only), the areas covered, and the hours of enforcement. A written agreement protects both the property owner and the towing company.

**Step 3: Install compliant signage.** Post signs at every entrance per ARS 9-499.05 and HB 2269 requirements. Photograph each sign with a timestamp for your records.

**Step 4: Establish parking rules and communicate to tenants/residents.** Clearly define parking rules in lease agreements, HOA CC&Rs, or tenant handbooks. Provide written notice to all current residents before enforcement begins.

**Step 5: Document everything.** Maintain records of the towing authorization agreement, signage photographs, tenant notifications, and individual tow records. This documentation is your defense against any claims of improper towing.

**Step 6: Verify ongoing compliance.** Periodically check that signs are intact, visible, and accurate. Update signs immediately if you change towing companies. Audit your towing company's documentation practices.

## Part 2: What Vehicle Owners Need to Know

### Your Rights When Your Car Is Towed from Private Property

If your vehicle has been towed from a private property in Arizona, you have specific legal rights:

#### Right to 24-Hour Release
The impound lot must release your vehicle at any time of day or night, any day of the week. They cannot tell you to "come back during business hours." If they refuse, they are violating ARS 9-499.05.

#### Right to Multiple Payment Methods
The impound lot must accept cash, debit cards, and credit cards. They cannot insist on cash only or refuse your credit card.

#### Right to an Itemized Receipt
You are entitled to a written, itemized breakdown of every fee you are charged. This includes the tow charge, daily storage, administrative fees, and any other charges.

#### Right to Retrieve Personal Belongings
You have the right to retrieve personal belongings from your impounded vehicle, even if you cannot afford to pay the full release fees immediately. The towing company cannot charge you for this access.

#### Right to Dispute an Improper Tow
If you believe the tow was illegal (no proper signage, no authorization, towed from a public street without law enforcement involvement), you can:

1. **File a complaint with the city** where the tow occurred
2. **Contact the Arizona Attorney General's Consumer Protection Division**
3. **Consult with an attorney** about recovering your fees plus potential damages
4. **Document everything** including photos of the location, signage (or lack thereof), and all receipts

#### Right to a Post-Impound Hearing (ARS 28-3512)

For law enforcement-ordered impounds, vehicle owners have the right to request a hearing within 10 days to challenge the impound. If the hearing officer determines the impound was not justified, you are entitled to reimbursement of all fees.

Note: This hearing right under ARS 28-3512 applies specifically to law enforcement-ordered impounds. For private property impounds, your recourse is through the civil court system or consumer protection agencies.

### Common Reasons Vehicles Are Towed from Private Property

Understanding why vehicles are towed can help you avoid the situation:

1. **No parking permit or authorization** -- Parking in a lot that requires permits without displaying one
2. **Expired registration** displayed on the vehicle
3. **Parking in a fire lane** -- This is both a safety violation and a towable offense
4. **Blocking a dumpster, loading zone, or emergency access**
5. **Parking in a handicap space without a valid placard** (ADA compliance)
6. **Parking in a reserved or assigned space** that belongs to another tenant
7. **Vehicle appears abandoned** (flat tires, extensive dust, no movement for extended period)
8. **Violating posted parking rules** (time limits, size restrictions, specific area prohibitions)
9. **Parked on the property after business hours** when signage restricts after-hours parking

### How to Avoid Being Towed from Private Property

- **Read all parking signs** before leaving your vehicle, including time restrictions and permit requirements
- **Display your parking permit** prominently if one is required
- **Do not park in fire lanes, handicap spaces (without a valid placard), or reserved spots**
- **Check with the property manager** if you are unsure about parking rules
- **Do not leave your vehicle for extended periods** without moving it

## Part 3: Special Situations

### HOA Towing in Arizona

Homeowner associations have the authority to tow vehicles that violate parking rules established in their CC&Rs (Covenants, Conditions & Restrictions). However, HOA towing is subject to additional considerations:

- The CC&Rs must specifically authorize towing as an enforcement mechanism
- The HOA must follow its own rules and procedures before authorizing a tow
- Proper signage per ARS 9-499.05 must be posted in HOA common areas
- The HOA board must have authorized the towing agreement (typically by board vote)
- Some Arizona HOA laws restrict towing from public streets adjacent to the community

For a comprehensive guide to HOA towing programs, see our [HOA towing program setup guide](/blog/hoa-towing-program-setup-guide).

### Apartment Complex Towing

Apartment complex towing is one of the most common forms of private property towing in Arizona. Key considerations:

- **Lease integration:** Parking rules should be incorporated into the lease agreement
- **Notice to tenants:** Tenants must be informed of parking rules before enforcement begins
- **Visitor parking:** Clear rules must exist for guest and visitor parking
- **Tow authorization:** The property manager (not individual tenants) authorizes tows
- **ARS compliance:** All ARS 9-499.05 requirements apply to apartment complexes

Learn more about apartment complex parking management in our [apartment complex parking management guide](/blog/apartment-complex-parking-management-guide).

### Abandoned Vehicle Removal

Arizona has specific procedures for abandoned vehicles on private property (ARS 28-4801 through 28-4850):

- A vehicle is generally considered abandoned if it has been left on private property for an extended period without authorization
- The property owner must follow specific notice and waiting period requirements before having an abandoned vehicle removed
- The towing company may initiate lien proceedings to recover storage costs if the vehicle is not claimed
- Unclaimed vehicles may eventually be sold at auction

See our detailed guide on [abandoned vehicle removal from private property in Arizona](/blog/abandoned-vehicle-removal-private-property-arizona).

### Vehicle Relocations (Non-Impound Towing)

Vehicle relocations are a distinct service from impound towing. In a relocation, vehicles are moved temporarily (not to an impound lot) to accommodate:

- Parking lot repaving or resealing
- Property maintenance or construction
- Special events requiring temporary lot clearing
- Emergency situations

Relocations have different legal requirements than impounds because the vehicle is not being seized or held for payment. Proper notice to vehicle owners (typically 48-72 hours advance notice) is required. Learn more about [vehicle relocation services](/services/vehicle-relocations).

## Part 4: Penalties for Non-Compliance

### For Property Owners

Property owners who authorize towing without proper compliance face potential liability including:

- **Reimbursement of all impound fees** to the vehicle owner
- **Compensatory damages** for any harm caused (missed work, transportation costs)
- **Punitive damages** in cases of particularly egregious or repeated violations
- **Attorney's fees** if the vehicle owner prevails in court
- **Regulatory penalties** from the municipality

### For Towing Companies

Towing companies that perform non-compliant tows face:

- **Loss of tow truck license** under ARS 28-4841
- **Municipal fines and penalties**
- **Civil liability for damages** to vehicle owners
- **Loss of contracts** with property management companies
- **Increased insurance premiums**

### For Vehicle Owners

Vehicle owners who refuse to pay legitimate impound fees or attempt to retrieve vehicles without proper authorization face:

- **Continued accumulation of storage fees**
- **Lien proceedings** by the towing company
- **Potential sale of the vehicle at auction** if not retrieved within the statutory period
- **Criminal charges** for attempting to forcibly retrieve a vehicle from an impound lot

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can my car be towed from private property without warning in Arizona?

If proper signage is posted per ARS 9-499.05 and HB 2269, the signs themselves serve as the legal warning. A property owner does not need to provide additional individual notice before each tow. However, if no compliant signs are posted, the tow may be illegal regardless of any other factors. For apartment tenants, parking rules should also be disclosed in the lease agreement.

### How long does a towing company have to notify police after a private property tow in Arizona?

Arizona law requires notification to law enforcement within a "reasonable time" after the tow. Most municipalities interpret this as within 30 minutes to 2 hours. In practice, most Phoenix-area towing companies use electronic reporting systems that notify police within minutes of the tow being completed.

### Can a towing company charge whatever they want for impound fees in Arizona?

No. Under ARS 9-499.05, municipalities have the authority to regulate towing and storage fees. Many Phoenix metro cities have set maximum fee schedules. Additionally, fees must be "reasonable," and the towing company must provide an itemized receipt. If you believe fees are excessive, check your city's towing fee ordinance and file a complaint with the city if the charges exceed the maximum.

### Can I sue if my car was towed illegally from private property in Arizona?

Yes. If your vehicle was towed without proper signage, without proper authorization, or in violation of any ARS 9-499.05 requirement, you may file a civil lawsuit against the towing company and/or the property owner. You may recover the impound fees you paid, consequential damages (transportation costs, missed work), and potentially attorney's fees. Consult with an Arizona consumer rights attorney for advice specific to your situation.

### What is the difference between a private property tow and a police-ordered tow in Arizona?

A private property tow is authorized by the property owner or manager to remove unauthorized vehicles from private property. A police-ordered tow is authorized by law enforcement, typically for vehicles involved in crimes, DUI stops, or serious traffic violations. Police-ordered tows under ARS 28-3511 can involve mandatory hold periods (up to 30 days), stricter release requirements, and the right to a post-impound hearing under ARS 28-3512. Private property tows have no mandatory hold period and must be released immediately upon payment.
